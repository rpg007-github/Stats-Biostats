01Descriptive Statistics

Descriptive statistics are just ways to describe a pile of numbers so you can understand them quickly. Instead of reading 100 test scores one by one, you want one or two numbers that tell the story.

There are two main questions: Where is the middle? and How spread out are the numbers?

Think of It This Way

Imagine 5 friends compare their heights: 5'2", 5'4", 5'5", 5'6", and 6'8". The mean (average) is pulled way up by the very tall friend. The median (middle value) = 5'5" and gives a better sense of "typical."

Worked Example — Mean vs. Median

Five students' scores: 60, 62, 65, 68, 95

Mean = (60+62+65+68+95) ÷ 5 = 350 ÷ 5 = 70

Median = middle value when sorted = 65

So what? The mean of 70 makes the class sound pretty average, but four out of five students actually scored below 70. The one high score (95) dragged the mean up. The median of 65 better represents how most students did.

Rule of thumb: Use the median when you have extreme outliers (like one very high or very low value).

Worked Example — Mode in Real Life

A shoe store tracks sizes sold in one week: 7, 8, 8, 9, 9, 9, 9, 10, 10, 11

Mode = 9 (appears 4 times — more than any other size)

Why it matters: The store manager does not need the average shoe size — averages do not come in boxes. The mode tells them which size to order the most of. Mode is the only measure of center that works for categories ("most popular color sold: blue").

Worked Example — Range vs. Standard Deviation

Two classes, same mean (75):

Class A scores: 73, 74, 75, 76, 77 — Range = 4, SD ≈ 1.6 (very consistent)

Class B scores: 50, 60, 75, 90, 100 — Range = 50, SD ≈ 20 (very spread out)

The mean of 75 is the same, but the experience of being in those classes is completely different. Standard deviation captures this. Class A students all cluster near 75. Class B has students who aced it and students who struggled. You always need a spread measure alongside the mean.

Measures of Central Tendency

The mean is the arithmetic average. The median is the exact middle value when data is sorted. The mode is the value that appears most often — it is the only measure you can use with categorical data (you cannot average "red" and "blue," but you can say "red is the most common").

Measures of Spread

The range = largest minus smallest value. Simple, but one extreme value wrecks it. The standard deviation (SD) measures how far scores typically fall from the mean. A small SD means scores are bunched together; a large SD means they are all over the place. The variance is just SD squared (SD²).

Standard Deviation (Sample)
s = √[ Σ(xᵢ − x̄)² / (n − 1) ]

Translation: subtract the mean from each score, square the result, add them all up, divide by (n − 1), then take the square root.

Worked Example — Calculating Standard Deviation

Scores: 70, 75, 80, 85, 90  |  Mean = 80

Step 1 — Subtract mean: −10, −5, 0, +5, +10

Step 2 — Square each: 100, 25, 0, 25, 100

Step 3 — Add squared values: 100+25+0+25+100 = 250

Step 4 — Divide by (n−1) = 4: 250 ÷ 4 = 62.5

Step 5 — Square root: √62.5 = 7.91

Meaning: On average, scores fall about ±7.9 points from the mean of 80.

Key Point

When data is skewed (like incomes with a few millionaires), the mean is pulled toward the outlier. Use the median for skewed data. Use the mean for symmetric data. This is why the news always reports median household income, not mean.

Why divide by n − 1 (not n) when computing sample standard deviation? Because you are using the sample mean as an estimate of the population mean, which introduces a slight bias. Dividing by n − 1 corrects for this "Bessel's correction" and gives an unbiased estimator of the population variance. When computing a population standard deviation (when you have every data point, not just a sample), divide by n.

The interquartile range (IQR) = Q3 − Q1, spanning the middle 50% of data. It is resistant to outliers and is the spread measure paired with the median. A z-score transforms any value into units of standard deviation: z = (x − μ) / σ. Values beyond ±3 are considered extreme outliers in many contexts.

Worked Example — Identifying the Right Summary Statistics

Scenario: A hospital records length of stay (days) for 200 patients: most stay 2–5 days, but 10 patients required 45–90 day ICU stays.

Median stay: 3 days    Mean stay: 7.4 days    IQR: 2–5 days

What to report and why: The 10 ICU patients are legitimate but extreme values. They inflate the mean to 7.4, which misrepresents 95% of patients. The correct report is "median stay = 3 days (IQR: 2–5)" because it reflects the typical patient. The ICU outliers should be described separately. Journals in clinical medicine strongly prefer median ± IQR for skewed data.

MCAT/Exam Watch

In a perfectly symmetric, unimodal distribution: mean = median = mode. Right-skewed data (long tail to the right, like incomes): mean > median > mode. Left-skewed data (long tail to the left): mean < median < mode. You will be asked to identify the direction of skew from a histogram and predict which measure is largest.

02Probability Foundations

Probability is a number between 0 and 1 that tells you how likely something is. 0 = impossible. 1 = certain. 0.5 = 50/50 (like flipping a fair coin).

Worked Example — Basic Probability

A bag has 3 red marbles and 7 blue marbles (10 total).

P(red) = 3/10 = 0.30 = 30%

P(blue) = 7/10 = 0.70 = 70%

P(red) + P(blue) = 1.0 ✓ (all possibilities must add to 1)

Two events are independent if one does not affect the other (like flipping a coin twice — the first flip does not change the second). They are mutually exclusive if they cannot happen at the same time (you cannot roll a 2 and a 5 on the same die roll).

The addition rule: P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A and B). You subtract P(A and B) to avoid double counting. If A and B are mutually exclusive, P(A and B) = 0, so P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B).

The multiplication rule: P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B|A). If independent: P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B).

Worked Example — Multiplication Rule

Question: In a class of 30 students, 12 are pre-med, 8 have a GPA above 3.8. Are these independent? If you pick a student randomly, what is P(pre-med AND GPA above 3.8) if independent?

P(pre-med) = 12/30 = 0.40    P(GPA > 3.8) = 8/30 = 0.267

If independent: P(both) = 0.40 × 0.267 = 0.107 (about 10.7%)

Reality check: Pre-med students often have higher GPAs, so these events are probably NOT independent in real life.

Conditional Probability

Conditional probability P(A|B) = "probability of A given that B already happened." Formula: P(A|B) = P(A and B) / P(B).

Worked Example — Conditional Probability in Medicine

Scenario: Among 1,000 patients at a clinic, 200 smoke and 800 do not. Of the 200 smokers, 60 develop lung disease. Of the 800 non-smokers, 40 develop lung disease.

P(lung disease | smoker) = 60 / 200 = 0.30 (30%)

P(lung disease | non-smoker) = 40 / 800 = 0.05 (5%)

Interpretation: Smokers are 6 times more likely to develop lung disease. This is the basic logic behind relative risk — conditional probability is how we compare disease rates between groups in epidemiology.

Worked Example — Addition Rule (Overlapping Events)

In a class of 40 students: 18 study biology, 15 study chemistry, 8 study both.

P(bio OR chem) = P(bio) + P(chem) − P(both)

= 18/40 + 15/40 − 8/40 = 25/40 = 0.625

Why subtract the overlap? Those 8 students who take both were counted once in "bio" and once in "chem." Subtract them once to correct for the double count.

Bayes' Theorem

Bayes' Theorem lets you update a probability when you get new evidence. It is the foundation of medical diagnostic reasoning.

Bayes' Theorem
P(Disease | Positive Test) = [P(Positive | Disease) × P(Disease)] / P(Positive)
Worked Example — Why Rare Diseases Fool Us

Setup: A disease affects 1 in 1,000 people (prevalence = 0.1%). A test is 99% sensitive (detects 99% of sick people) and 99% specific (correctly excludes 99% of healthy people).

Intuition says: 99% accurate — so a positive test must mean I'm sick, right?

In 100,000 people: 100 have the disease. Test finds 99 of them (true positives). Of the 99,900 healthy people, 1% get false positives = 999 false positives.

So if you test positive: P(sick | positive) = 99 / (99 + 999) = 99/1,098 = 9%

Lesson: A positive test on a rare disease still mostly means you do NOT have it. This is the key reason positive screening results are followed by confirmatory tests.

03Probability Distributions

A probability distribution is a picture (or table) showing how likely each possible outcome is. The most famous one is the normal distribution — the bell curve you have probably seen.

Think of It This Way

If you measured the height of every adult in the U.S. and made a histogram, it would look like a bell — most people are near the average, fewer people are very short or very tall. That bell shape is the normal distribution.

The 68–95–99.7 Rule

In any normal distribution: 68% of data falls within 1 standard deviation of the mean. 95% falls within 2 SD. 99.7% falls within 3 SD. If a value is more than 2 SD away, it is unusual. More than 3 SD — it is rare.

Worked Example — Using the 68–95–99.7 Rule

Exam scores in a large class are normally distributed: mean = 75, SD = 8.

68% of students scored between: 75 − 8 = 67 and 75 + 8 = 67 to 83

95% of students scored between: 75 − 16 = 59 and 75 + 16 = 59 to 91

99.7% of students scored between: 75 − 24 = 51 and 75 + 24 = 51 to 99

A student who scored 59 is at exactly 2 SD below the mean — below 97.5% of the class. Unusual, but still within the expected range of scores.

A student who scored 99 is 3 SD above the mean — in the top 0.15% of the class. Genuinely exceptional.

Z-Scores

A z-score standardizes any value. It tells you how many standard deviations above or below the mean a value sits.

Z-Score Formula
z = (x − μ) / σ

x = your value, μ = mean, σ = standard deviation

Worked Example — Comparing Two Different Scales

A student scores 82 on a biology exam (class mean = 75, SD = 5) and 720 on the SAT Math (mean = 530, SD = 100).

Biology z-score: (82 − 75) / 5 = +1.40

SAT z-score: (720 − 530) / 100 = +1.90

Conclusion: The student performed relatively better on the SAT (+1.90 SD) than on the biology exam (+1.40 SD). Without z-scores, you cannot compare a 720 to an 82 — they are on completely different scales.

Other Key Distributions

Common probability distributions and when to use them
DistributionUse It WhenKey Feature
BinomialCounting yes/no outcomes in n fixed trialsNeed fixed n, same p each trial, independence
PoissonCounting rare events in a fixed time/spaceλ = average rate; events are independent
t-distributionSmall samples (n < 30) or unknown σFatter tails than normal; becomes normal as n grows
Chi-square (χ²)Testing categorical dataAlways positive; shape changes with degrees of freedom

The normal distribution is fully described by two parameters: mean (μ) and standard deviation (σ). The standard normal distribution has μ = 0 and σ = 1. Converting to z-scores lets you use standard normal tables to find exact probabilities.

The t-distribution has heavier tails than the normal — it assigns more probability to extreme values. This is appropriate when estimating population parameters from small samples, because we have more uncertainty about the true population SD. As sample size grows, the t-distribution converges on the standard normal.

Common Student Mistake

Using z-tests when t-tests are required. Use a z-test only when the population standard deviation (σ) is known — which almost never happens in real research. If you estimated SD from your sample, you must use a t-test. With n > 30, the difference is small, but the t-test is always the safer, more technically correct choice.

Worked Example — Binomial Probability

Question: A drug has a 30% success rate. You give it to 5 patients. What is the probability that exactly 2 of them respond?

Use the binomial formula: P(X = k) = C(n,k) × p^k × (1−p)^(n−k)

P(X = 2) = C(5,2) × 0.30² × 0.70³ = 10 × 0.09 × 0.343 = 0.309

About a 31% chance that exactly 2 out of 5 patients respond.

04Sampling & Estimation

You cannot test every person in the world, so instead you test a sample — a smaller group — and use what you learn to make an educated guess about everyone (the population).

Think of It This Way

A chef tastes one spoonful of soup to know if the whole pot needs salt. The spoonful is the sample. The whole pot is the population. This only works if the spoonful was well-mixed (random).

The most important thing: your sample must be random. A bad sample (like only asking your friends) gives answers that do not represent everyone.

Sampling Methods

Comparison of common sampling strategies

Confidence Intervals — The Plain Version

A 95% confidence interval gives a range that, using this method, would capture the true population value 95% of the time if you repeated the study many times. It is NOT a 95% chance the true value is in this specific interval — the true value is fixed; the interval is what varies.

Worked Example — Reading a Confidence Interval

A study measures resting heart rate in 36 students. Mean = 72 bpm, SD = 8.

SE = 8 / √36 = 8 / 6 = 1.33. Using t* ≈ 2.03:

95% CI = 72 ± 2.03 × 1.33 = 72 ± 2.7 = (69.3, 74.7) bpm

Correct interpretation: "We are 95% confident the true mean resting heart rate is between 69.3 and 74.7 bpm." We are NOT saying there is a 95% chance the true mean lies in this range (the true mean either is or is not in this interval — it is fixed).

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT)

The Central Limit Theorem states that the sampling distribution of the sample mean approaches a normal distribution as n increases, regardless of the population's original shape. For most populations, n ≥ 30 is sufficient. This is the theoretical backbone of virtually every hypothesis test.

Standard Error of the Mean
SE = σ / √n   (or s / √n when σ is unknown)
Worked Example — How Sample Size Affects Precision

Population SD of exam scores = 12 points. How does SE change?

n = 9: SE = 12/3 = 4.0    n = 36: SE = 12/6 = 2.0    n = 144: SE = 12/12 = 1.0

Pattern: To cut SE in half, you must quadruple n. This is the diminishing returns law of sample size — going from 9 to 36 students halves your uncertainty, but going from 900 to 3,600 also only halves it again. This is why huge clinical trials are expensive and why researchers always balance precision against budget.

The margin of error in a confidence interval = critical value × SE. Wider CI = less precision (larger SE or higher confidence level). Narrower CI = more precision (larger n or lower confidence level). There is always a tradeoff between confidence level and width.

05Hypothesis Testing

Hypothesis testing is a formal way to use data to answer yes/no questions. Does a new drug work? Is one teaching method better than another?

The Courtroom Analogy

In a trial, you assume the defendant is innocent until proven guilty. You need evidence to convict. In statistics, the null hypothesis (H₀) is "innocent" — nothing unusual is happening. The alternative hypothesis (Hₐ) is the "guilty" claim you are trying to prove. You never declare the defendant "innocent" — only "not enough evidence to convict." Same in statistics: you never "accept" the null, you only "fail to reject" it.

The p-value is the probability of seeing data as extreme as yours if the null hypothesis were true. A small p-value (usually below 0.05) means the data would be very unusual if the null were true — so you reject the null.

The Steps — Applied to a Real Scenario

Worked Example — Does a New Drug Lower Blood Pressure?

Step 1 — State the hypotheses:

H₀: The drug has no effect (mean change = 0 mmHg)

Hₐ: The drug lowers blood pressure (mean change < 0 mmHg)

Step 2 — Set alpha: α = 0.05 (accept a 5% chance of a false alarm)

Step 3 — Collect data: 40 patients, mean change = −5.2 mmHg, SD = 8.1 mmHg

Step 4 — Calculate test statistic: t = −5.2 / (8.1/√40) = −5.2 / 1.28 = −4.06

Step 5 — Find p-value: p < 0.001

Step 6 — Decide: p (0.001) < α (0.05) → Reject H₀. Strong evidence the drug lowers blood pressure.

Type I and Type II Errors

The two ways hypothesis testing can go wrong
Error TypeWhat HappenedReal-World AnalogyRate
Type I (false positive)Rejected H₀ when it was actually trueConvicted an innocent personα (usually 0.05)
Type II (false negative)Failed to reject H₀ when it was actually falseLet a guilty person go freeβ
Power = 1 − β

Statistical power = the probability of correctly detecting a real effect. Aim for power ≥ 0.80. Power increases when: sample size increases, effect size is larger, or alpha is set higher.

The p-value does NOT tell you the probability that H₀ is true. It tells you P(data this extreme or more extreme | H₀ is true). The distinction is critical. A p = 0.03 means that IF the null is true, there is a 3% chance of seeing data this extreme — not a 3% chance the null is true.

Statistical significance ≠ practical significance. With n = 10,000, a drug might significantly reduce blood pressure by 0.5 mmHg (p = 0.001). That is clinically meaningless. Always report effect sizes (Cohen's d, r², NNT) alongside p-values.

Worked Example — One-Sided vs. Two-Sided Tests

Two-sided test: "Does the drug change blood pressure at all?" Hₐ: μ ≠ 0. The critical region is split: 2.5% in each tail.

One-sided test: "Does the drug lower blood pressure?" Hₐ: μ < 0. The entire critical region is in one tail (5%).

Caution: Choosing a one-sided test after seeing your data is p-hacking. The test direction must be committed to before data collection based on scientific reasoning, not convenience. One-sided tests have more power but are only appropriate when a result in the opposite direction would be meaningless or impossible.

Multiple Comparisons Problem

If you run 20 tests at α = 0.05, you expect 1 false positive purely by chance. The probability of at least one false positive = 1 − (0.95)²⁰ = 64%. Solutions: Bonferroni correction (divide α by number of tests) or control the false discovery rate (Benjamini-Hochberg). Any study reporting many comparisons without a correction method should be read skeptically.

06Common Statistical Tests

Different questions need different statistical tests — just like different tools in a toolbox. Choosing the wrong test gives you wrong answers, even with correct data.

Three Questions to Pick Your Test

1. How many groups? (1, 2, or 3+)    2. What kind of data? (numbers vs. categories)    3. Are groups the same people or different people? (paired vs. independent)

Those three answers almost always point you to the right test.

Statistical test selection guide
SituationTest to UseWhat It Asks
One sample mean vs. known valueOne-sample t-testIs my sample mean different from some target?
Two independent groups, numeric dataIndependent t-testAre two groups' means different?
Same people measured twice (before/after)Paired t-testIs the average change different from zero?
Three or more groups, numeric dataOne-way ANOVAIs at least one group's mean different?
Two categorical variablesChi-square testAre these two categories associated?

Chi-Square Test — Step by Step

Worked Example — Is Exercise Associated With Flu?

Data: 200 people — 100 who exercise regularly and 100 who do not. Among exercisers: 20 got flu, 80 did not. Non-exercisers: 40 got flu, 60 did not.

If there is no association (null hypothesis): Expected flu rate = 30% in both groups. So expected counts = 30 flu / 70 no-flu in each group.

χ² = (20−30)²/30 + (80−70)²/70 + (40−30)²/30 + (60−70)²/70

= 100/30 + 100/70 + 100/30 + 100/70 = 3.33 + 1.43 + 3.33 + 1.43 = 9.52

Degrees of freedom = (rows − 1)(columns − 1) = 1 × 1 = 1

Critical value at df=1, α=0.05 = 3.84. Since 9.52 > 3.84: Reject the null. Exercise is significantly associated with lower flu rates.

The independent t-test assumes: continuous data, independence of observations, normality of both groups (less critical with n > 30), and approximately equal variances (homoscedasticity). Levene's test checks the variance assumption. If variances are unequal, use Welch's t-test, which does not assume equal variances.

Independent Two-Sample T-Statistic
t = (x̄₁ − x̄₂) / √(s₁²/n₁ + s₂²/n₂)
Worked Example — Paired vs. Independent t-Test

Scenario: Researchers test whether a relaxation intervention lowers blood pressure. They measure BP before and after in the same 20 patients.

Wrong test: Independent t-test (ignores the pairing — treats 40 measurements as if from 40 different people).

Right test: Paired t-test (analyzes the difference score for each person, then tests if the mean difference ≠ 0).

Why it matters: Pairing removes between-person variability (some people naturally have higher BP). The paired test has more power because it controls for this noise. If the mean difference = −8 mmHg, SD of differences = 6, n = 20: t = −8 / (6/√20) = −8 / 1.34 = −5.97, p < 0.001.

For chi-square tests, all expected cell frequencies must be ≥ 5. If this assumption is violated (small samples or rare events), use Fisher's exact test, which calculates the exact probability rather than using the chi-square approximation.

07Correlation & Regression

Correlation measures whether two variables tend to move together. As height increases, weight tends to increase too — that is a positive correlation. As temperature goes up, hot chocolate sales go down — that is a negative correlation.

Most Important Concept in This Section

Correlation does NOT mean causation. Ice cream sales and drowning deaths are positively correlated — but eating ice cream does not cause drowning. Both go up in summer because of heat. A third factor (the season) drives both. This is called a confounding variable.

The Pearson correlation coefficient (r) ranges from −1 to +1. As a rough guide: |r| < 0.3 is weak; 0.3–0.7 is moderate; > 0.7 is strong. The sign tells the direction; the number tells the strength.

Worked Example — Interpreting r and r²

Study hours vs. exam scores: r = 0.80

r² = 0.80² = 0.64

What r = 0.80 tells you: Strong positive relationship — more study hours → higher scores.

What r² = 0.64 tells you: 64% of the variation in exam scores can be explained by variation in study hours. The other 36% is explained by other factors: sleep quality, anxiety, prior knowledge, how well the test matched the material studied.

r² is called the coefficient of determination — it tells you the practical usefulness of the relationship.

Simple Linear Regression

Regression Line
ŷ = b₀ + b₁x

b₀ = y-intercept (predicted Y when X = 0)    b₁ = slope (change in Y for each 1-unit increase in X)

Worked Example — Reading a Regression Equation

Predicted GPA = 1.5 + 0.08 × (study hours per week)

Slope = 0.08: Each additional hour of study per week predicts a 0.08 increase in GPA.

20 hrs/week → GPA = 1.5 + 1.6 = 3.1

30 hrs/week → GPA = 1.5 + 2.4 = 3.9

Important: Do not predict GPA for 60 hours/week — that is outside the data range. The relationship might not hold there.

Regression assumptions (LINE): Linearity (relationship is linear); Independence of residuals; Normality of residuals; Equal variance (homoscedasticity — residuals have constant spread across all X values). Violations require transformation or different modeling approaches.

Multiple regression extends the model: ŷ = b₀ + b₁x₁ + b₂x₂ + … + bₙxₙ. Each coefficient represents the effect of that variable while holding all others constant. This is how researchers control for confounders statistically.

Worked Example — Multicollinearity Warning

Problem: A researcher regresses blood pressure on both age and years of smoking. Age and smoking years are highly correlated (older people have smoked longer).

Result: Each coefficient becomes unreliable because the model cannot distinguish the independent effect of age from the effect of smoking years. This is multicollinearity.

Solutions: Remove one predictor, combine them into a composite, or use ridge regression. Variance Inflation Factor (VIF) > 10 flags problematic multicollinearity.

08ANOVA — Analysis of Variance

ANOVA answers the question: "Are the means of three or more groups different from each other?" It is like a t-test, but for more than two groups at the same time.

Think of It This Way

You have three study groups: Group A (no music), Group B (classical music), Group C (pop music). You give all three groups the same test. Are their average scores actually different, or are the differences just random chance? ANOVA answers that.

Why not just run three separate t-tests? Because each test has a 5% chance of a false alarm, and running three tests means three chances for a false alarm. ANOVA controls that by doing it all at once.

The F-Statistic
F = MS_between / MS_within

MS = Mean Square = SS / df. A large F means between-group differences dominate the noise.

Worked Example — Interpreting ANOVA Output

Scenario: Three diets tested on 30 people (10 per group). Weight loss after 3 months:

Diet A: mean = 4 lbs    Diet B: mean = 7 lbs    Diet C: mean = 12 lbs

ANOVA F(2, 27) = 8.43, p = 0.001

Interpretation: p = 0.001 < 0.05, so at least one diet produces significantly different weight loss than the others. But ANOVA does NOT tell us which pair(s) differ.

Next step: Run a post-hoc test (like Tukey HSD) to identify the specific pairs. Result might show: A vs. C is significant, B vs. C is significant, but A vs. B is not.

ANOVA assumptions: independence of observations; approximate normality within each group; homogeneity of variance across groups (tested with Levene's or Bartlett's test). ANOVA is reasonably robust to mild normality violations with n > 15 per group, but variance heterogeneity is more problematic.

Degrees of freedom: df_between = k − 1 (k = number of groups); df_within = N − k (N = total observations). Total df = N − 1.

Two-way ANOVA tests two independent variables simultaneously and also tests their interaction. An interaction means the effect of variable A depends on the level of variable B. For example, a drug might lower blood pressure in women but raise it in men — that is a significant drug × sex interaction, and it would be missed by two separate one-way ANOVAs.

Worked Example — Why Multiple T-Tests Are Wrong

With 4 groups, you need C(4,2) = 6 pairwise t-tests.

P(at least one false positive) = 1 − (0.95)⁶ = 1 − 0.735 = 26.5%

You intended a 5% Type I error rate. You got a 26.5% rate. Your entire study's credibility is compromised. ANOVA + post-hoc tests control this by maintaining the family-wise error rate at α = 0.05.

09Non-Parametric Tests

Most statistical tests assume your data follows a bell curve. But what if it does not? Or what if your data is ranked (like "rate your pain from 1–10") rather than measured precisely? That is when you use non-parametric tests — they make fewer assumptions about the shape of your data.

Think of It This Way

Regular (parametric) tests are like a fancy scale that gives exact readings — but it breaks down if you put something weird on it. Non-parametric tests are like a simple ranking system — less powerful, but they work even when the data is messy.

Worked Example — Ranks Replace Raw Numbers

Five patients' pain scores (1–10 scale) before treatment: 3, 7, 9, 2, 8

Step 1 — Rank them lowest to highest: 2→rank 1, 3→rank 2, 7→rank 3, 8→rank 4, 9→rank 5

Why rank? We do not know if the gap between pain scores of 7 and 8 is the same as the gap between 2 and 3. By converting to ranks, we only claim that 8 is worse than 7, not how much worse. This makes the test valid even with a scale that may not be perfectly equal-interval.

Worked Example — When to Choose Non-Parametric

Scenario: Nurses rate post-surgery pain for 10 patients before and after a new protocol. Ratings are 1–10 (ordinal scale). Small sample, clear non-normal distribution.

Before: 8, 7, 9, 6, 8, 7, 9, 8, 7, 6    After: 4, 3, 5, 4, 3, 4, 5, 4, 3, 4

Wrong choice: Paired t-test (pain ratings are ordinal — the difference between 3 and 4 may not equal the difference between 8 and 9)

Right choice: Wilcoxon signed-rank test (ranks the differences without assuming equal intervals between scale values)

Non-parametric tests replace raw data values with their ranks. The Mann-Whitney U test checks whether values from Group 1 tend to have higher or lower ranks than values from Group 2. By working with ranks, the test is insensitive to extreme outliers and does not require a distributional assumption.

The tradeoff: non-parametric tests are less powerful than their parametric counterparts when parametric assumptions are actually met. A t-test on normally distributed data will detect real effects more often than a Mann-Whitney U on the same data. If your data truly meets parametric assumptions, the parametric test is preferred.

Decision Rule

Continuous data + n > 20 per group + roughly symmetric → parametric test. Ordinal data, small n, heavy-tailed distribution, or marked outliers → non-parametric. In doubt: run both. If they agree (both p < 0.05 or both p > 0.05), report the parametric. If they disagree, report the non-parametric and note the discrepancy.

10Study Design & Biostatistics

How you design a study determines what you can and cannot conclude. The most important distinction: does your study prove cause or just show association?

The Gold Standard

A randomized controlled trial (RCT) randomly assigns people to treatment or control groups. Because assignment is random, the groups are similar in every way except the treatment — so any difference in outcomes must be caused by the treatment. All other study designs can only show that two things happen together, not that one causes the other.

Worked Example — Observational vs. Experimental

Observational study: Researchers notice that people who drink coffee have fewer cases of Parkinson's disease. They conclude coffee might be protective.

Why they cannot say "coffee prevents Parkinson's": Coffee drinkers might also exercise more, smoke less, or differ in dozens of other ways. Any of those differences could explain the lower Parkinson's rate. This is a confounding variable.

To prove causation: Randomly assign 10,000 people to "drink 2 cups coffee/day" vs. "no coffee" for 20 years. Compare Parkinson's rates. Now you know coffee is the only systematic difference. This is an RCT — expensive and difficult, but the only way to establish cause.

Study designs ranked by strength of evidence (strongest first)
DesignDescriptionCan Prove Causation?
Randomized controlled trial (RCT)Randomly assign to treatment or controlYes — gold standard
Cohort study (prospective)Follow exposed vs. unexposed groups forward in timeNo — only association
Case-control studyCompare people with the disease to people without; look back at exposuresNo — only association
Cross-sectionalMeasure exposure and outcome at the same timeNo — cannot determine which came first
Case report/seriesDescribe one or a few patients in detailNo — no comparison group
Worked Example — The Classic Confounder

Observation: Coffee drinkers have higher rates of lung cancer.

Should people quit coffee? Not yet. Coffee drinkers are also more likely to smoke. Smoking is associated with both coffee drinking (the exposure) and lung cancer (the outcome). It is a confounder.

After controlling for smoking: The association between coffee and lung cancer largely disappears. It was not the coffee — it was the cigarettes that traveled with the coffee.

Blinding prevents observer and participant bias. Single-blind = participant does not know assignment. Double-blind = neither participant nor researcher knows. Triple-blind = the statistician analyzing data also does not know group assignments until after analysis. Randomization + double-blinding = highest quality evidence in a clinical trial.

11Diagnostic Testing & Epidemiology

When doctors test for a disease, the test is not perfect. It can miss real cases (false negative) or flag healthy people (false positive). Understanding how good a test really is involves two key concepts: sensitivity and specificity.

Simple Memory Hook

Sensitive test: Very good at finding sick people. If you test negative, you probably are NOT sick (rules OUT disease). Think: a smoke alarm — very sensitive, goes off at even tiny amounts of smoke.

Specific test: Very good at confirming healthy people. If you test positive, you probably DO have the disease (rules IN disease). Think: a DNA test — very specific, rarely points to the wrong person.

Sensitivity, specificity, and predictive values — formulas and plain-English meanings
MeasureFormulaPlain English
SensitivityTP / (TP + FN)Of everyone who is sick, what fraction does the test catch?
SpecificityTN / (TN + FP)Of everyone who is healthy, what fraction does the test correctly clear?
Positive Predictive Value (PPV)TP / (TP + FP)If I test positive, what is the probability I am actually sick?
Negative Predictive Value (NPV)TN / (TN + FN)If I test negative, what is the probability I am actually healthy?
Worked Example — Calculating All Four Values

Strep throat rapid test: 200 patients tested. TP = 45, FP = 10, FN = 5, TN = 140.

Sensitivity = 45 / (45+5) = 45/50 = 90%    (catches 90% of strep cases)

Specificity = 140 / (140+10) = 140/150 = 93.3%    (correctly clears 93.3% of non-strep)

PPV = 45 / (45+10) = 45/55 = 81.8%    (if positive, 82% chance you have strep)

NPV = 140 / (140+5) = 140/145 = 96.6%    (if negative, 97% chance you do not have strep)

SnNOut and SpPIn

SnNOut: High Snsitivity + Negative result = rules Out disease. Use for screening.

SpPIn: High Specificity + Positive result = rules In disease. Use for confirmation.

Worked Example — Relative Risk and NNT

Cohort study: 1,000 smokers and 1,000 non-smokers followed for 10 years. 150 smokers develop heart disease; 30 non-smokers develop it.

RR = (150/1000) / (30/1000) = 0.15 / 0.03 = 5.0

Interpretation: Smokers are 5 times more likely to develop heart disease. RR > 1 = increased risk; RR < 1 = protective; RR = 1 = no difference. The 95% CI for RR must not include 1 for the result to be statistically significant.

NNT example: A drug reduces heart attack risk from 8% (control) to 5% (treatment). ARR = 8% − 5% = 3%. NNT = 1/0.03 = 33. You must treat 33 patients to prevent one heart attack. NNT helps translate statistical findings into clinical meaning.

PPV and Prevalence

PPV depends heavily on prevalence. Even a highly accurate test has poor PPV when applied to a low-prevalence population — most positives will be false positives (see the Bayes' Theorem worked example in Section 02). This is why population-wide screening for rare diseases requires extremely specific confirmatory tests before action is taken.
